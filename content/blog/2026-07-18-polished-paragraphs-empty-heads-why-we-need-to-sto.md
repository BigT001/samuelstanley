I spent my morning reviewing a pull request from an intern at our Gbagada workspace. The code looked incredibly clean. Beautifully structured, comments in all the right places, not a single formatting error. But when I asked him why he chose a specific database query pattern over a simpler search, he froze. He had no idea. He’d let a prompt do the heavy lifting, and the moment the code broke, he was completely lost.

This isn't just a developer issue. It’s the exact same crisis unfolding in our universities right now. 

Recent research on how students are using AI writing tools in 2026 shows a massive shift. What used to take days of sweating over library books, drafting outlines, and crying over bibliographies is now happening in thirty seconds. Students are using LLMs to summarize heavy academic papers, map out essay structures, and clean up their grammar. 

On paper, this sounds like a massive win. But in reality, we are building a dangerous shortcut.

![Struggling with the build](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### The Illusion of "Good" Work

As someone who builds software, I know how easy it is to mistake a polished user interface for a robust system. If the CSS looks beautiful, you assume the database backend is solid. 

The same thing is happening with student essays. AI tools are masters at generating smooth, grammatically flawless paragraphs. But polished writing is not the same thing as deep understanding. 

When you bypass the painful process of organizing your thoughts, debating your own points, and deciding what evidence actually matters, you miss the actual education. Writing isn't just a way to show what you know; it is the process through which you figure out what you think. If you let an LLM write the entire draft, you’re essentially skipping the gym and hiring someone else to lift the weights for you. You don't get stronger that way.

### The Danger of Untested Code (and Untried Facts)

In my line of work, we talk about "hallucinations" as a technical bug. In an academic setting, a hallucination is just a fancy word for a lie. 

Students who copy and paste AI-generated paragraphs without double-checking the facts are running uncompiled code in production. They risk submitting essays with fabricated historical dates, fake citations, and completely made-up statistics. 

I’ve met young guys in Akure and Owerri who are trying to build cool startups while managing their university coursework. To survive the hustle and escape the crushing weight of "Sapa", they use these AI tools to fast-track their assignments. I get the pressure. When the power grid collapses for the third time in a week and you have to run your generator on expensive fuel just to finish a term paper, you want the fastest route out. But cutting corners on basic research backfires the moment you have to defend your project in front of a real panel.

![The hustle is real](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550005810-ca9161a0215a?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Treat AI Like a Junior Dev, Not the Tech Lead

We shouldn’t ban these tools. That’s a losing battle. "No gree for anybody" means adapting, not hiding from the future. 

The trick is changing how we view the technology. 

If you are a student, treat AI like your junior developer. Use it to do the grunt work. Let it clean up your messy formatting, suggest three different ways to structure a difficult paragraph, or explain a complex mathematical concept that your lecturer glossed over during a two-hour slide presentation. 

But do not let it write your thesis. Do not let it make your arguments. 

If you can’t defend the core logic of your essay or your codebase without a screen in front of you, you haven't actually built anything. You're just a wrapper for someone else's API.
