Woke up this morning, coffee in hand, ready to tackle a tricky frontend bug that’s been nagging me. Then I saw the news: PayPal Ventures, their venture capital arm, is shutting down, and with it, the much-hyped USD 100M commitment to Africa. My first thought? *Sigh.* My second thought? *Ehen. I dey expect am.*

### Déjà Vu, But Make It Tech

Honestly, it’s not surprising. PayPal’s relationship with Africa, especially Nigeria, has always felt like a series of half-measures. They locked us out of receiving funds for 22 years because of "fraud risks," then came back with partnerships that felt more like bandaids than genuine solutions. I remember tinkering with e-commerce sites back in the day, wishing for seamless PayPal integration, only to hit that wall of "outbound payments only." It felt like a deliberate choice to keep us on the sidelines. To see them make a big splash about a USD 100M fund just months ago, then yank the rug out now? It just screams 'lack of understanding' or 'lack of genuine commitment'.

It's a stark reminder that when these global players talk about "long-term bets" in "far-flung markets," sometimes it's just corporate speak for "we'll try it if it's easy, but cut it the moment things get tight." For us developers, for founders trying to build real products in places like Onitsha or Port Harcourt, that kind of instability isn’t just bad press – it means a potential funding avenue just vanished. That's real money that could have fueled innovation, hired local talent, and built solutions tailored to our realities.

![A person coding on a laptop, surrounded by other monitors with code.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### When 'Luxury' Trumps 'Opportunity'

The article says new leadership at PayPal sees venture investing, especially in markets like ours, as a "luxury it can no longer afford." *Luxury?* Building an ecosystem, backing local innovation, bridging payment gaps – that's a luxury? For them, maybe. For us, it’s about solving real problems, creating jobs, and driving economic growth.

I’m thinking about the countless pitches I’ve heard, the late nights debugging, the sheer grit it takes to build a startup here in Nigeria. Every potential investor, every strategic partnership, feels like a lifeline. When a player as big as PayPal dangles USD 100M, it creates hope, it shapes strategies. Then, when it vanishes, it's not just a footnote in a corporate earnings report. It's a broken promise that leaves founders scrambling, having to re-evaluate their runway, or worse, put their dreams on hold.

### We Moved On, They’re Just Catching Up (Or Not)

The funny thing is, while PayPal was dilly-dallying, Africa’s fintech scene didn't just wait around. We got busy. M-Pesa in Kenya? A titan. Nigerian companies like Paystack (acquired by Stripe), Flutterwave, and even Paga – who PayPal just partnered with, ironically – have scaled massively. Our local champions aren't just surviving; they're thriving, building robust, user-friendly solutions that actually understand the local context.

Nigeria alone saw NGN 1.07 quadrillion (that's USD 754 billion, mind you) in domestic payments volume in 2024. Over 430 fintech firms are operating in this space. That's not a market waiting for someone to rescue it; that's a market forging its own path, fueled by ingenuity and a "no gree for anybody" spirit.

![A close-up of financial data on a screen, showing charts and numbers.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1526304640581-d334cd06f69d?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Build Anyway. Build Smarter.

This news, for me, isn't a discouragement. It's a reaffirmation. It reminds us that we can't always rely on foreign giants to swoop in and save the day. Our resilience, our ability to innovate with limited resources, is our greatest strength. We keep building. We focus on our users, on solving their unique problems, whether they're trying to send money to their family in a remote village near Jos or running an e-commerce store from a busy workstation in Gbagada.

This just means we need to be even smarter about where we seek investment, building products that are sustainable and valuable enough to attract capital from those who truly understand and believe in the long-term potential of the continent, not just its short-term quarterly reports. Let them retreat to their "core." We're busy building ours.
