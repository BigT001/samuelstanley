Three years ago, if you told me I’d be hosting a production database migration from a rented workspace in Akure without a single drop in connection, I would have laughed you out of the room. Back then, my stack was basically a prayer, two different MiFis from competing telcos, and my phone on standby for hotspot duties. Whenever the sky got slightly dark, my heart rate went up. 

If you build software in this country, you already know the tax. It’s not just the money; it’s the mental energy spent monitoring data balances and praying that local fiber cables don't get sliced open by road construction crews. 

When Starlink first showed up in early 2023, the pricing made everyone spit out their water. It felt like a luxury tool for tech bros who wanted to show off on Twitter. But fast forward to now, and the landscape has completely shifted. Almost 92,000 of us in Nigeria alone have paid for that rectangular dish. We didn't do it because we had money to throw away—we did it out of sheer survival.

![A developer trying to keep a stable connection while pushing code](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### The Last-Mile Lie We Lived With

For decades, the big telcos sold us the idea that building reliable internet takes ages because they have to dig up roads, lay physical pipes, and erect towers in every single corner of the country. If you lived outside the major tech hubs, you were basically treated as an afterthought. Trying to push a heavy Docker image or download a massive dependency package from a quiet suburb was enough to make you change careers.

Starlink basically bypassed that entire infrastructure headache. By sending signals straight from space, they showed how lazy our local providers had become with their "last-mile" delivery. 

It’s hilarious seeing the latest data showing Starlink as the second-largest ISP in Nigeria. Think about that for a second. An expensive, self-installed satellite kit is beating out local companies that have been on the ground for twenty years. The "No gree for anybody" spirit really applied here—instead of waiting for the local players to fix their networks, developers and small businesses saved up, bought the hardware, and took their power back.

### The Coping Mechanism of Our Telcos

What I find fascinating is how the big players are reacting. Initially, they tried to complain about licensing and regulations. Classic. But now, they are singing a different tune. They’re realizing that they can’t win this fight by locking horns, so they’re partnering up. 

MTN, Airtel, and the rest are suddenly realizing that instead of spending millions of dollars trying to lay fiber through difficult terrain, they can just plug into satellite backhauls to light up remote communities. It’s a massive reality check. They’re finally moving away from trying to own every single piece of the pipe and focusing on actually delivering service to people who need it.

![A view of the local hustle where reliable connectivity changes everything](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550005810-ca9161a0215a?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Fiber is Still King, But Only When It Works

Now, let's be honest for a second. If you talk to some old-school network engineers, they’ll tell you that satellite can't touch fiber when it comes to raw latency. And they’re technically right. If you’re building high-frequency trading bots or doing heavy multiplayer gaming where every millisecond counts, physical glass in the ground is always going to win. 

But for the rest of us? The guys running Gbagada workstations, the founders in Jos building remote startups, the creators in Owerri uploading video edits? We don’t need sub-5 millisecond latency. We just need the internet to stay on when it rains. We need to know that when we hit deploy, the connection won't drop at 99%.

This competition is the best thing that has happened to the Nigerian tech ecosystem in a long time. The local ISPs are finally feeling the heat. They can no longer afford to give us terrible customer service and blamed-on-the-weather downtimes because they know we now have options. 

If this pressure means my local fiber provider finally stops behaving like they are doing me a favor by letting me buy their data bundles, then I’m all for it. Keep the pressure on.
