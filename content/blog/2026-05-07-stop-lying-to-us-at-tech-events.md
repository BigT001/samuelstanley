I’m genuinely tired of attending tech conferences where every founder on stage sounds like they’ve been rehearsing with a PR team for a month. You know the vibe—everyone is "disrupting" something, "leveraging" something else, and claiming their uptime is 99.9% while we all know their NIP transfers have been hanging since yesterday morning. 

That’s why this announcement about "Spotlight by Technext" actually caught my eye. They’re claiming they want "honesty over performance." No panels. No fireside chats. Just 150 people in a room under Chatham House Rules trying to figure out why trust in Nigerian fintech feels so fragile right now.

![Building the tech is the easy part; keeping it running is the struggle](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### The Trust Gap isn't a "Strategy" Issue
As someone who spends most of my time looking at logs and wondering why a specific API endpoint is suddenly returning 500 errors, I can tell you that trust isn't built in a boardroom. It’s built at 2:00 AM in a Gbagada workstation when a developer is sweating over a database migration so that users don't wake up to "Sapa" because they can't access their own money.

When these guys talk about "Bridging Trust, Liquidity and User Reality," I hope they aren't just looking at charts. I hope they’re looking at the guy in Onitsha who stopped using fintech apps because a "pending" transaction took three weeks to reverse. That’s the user reality. If your code can't handle the chaotic energy of our local networks, your "fintech revolution" is just a fancy UI on a broken pipe.

### No Panels, Just Problems
The format they’re proposing—a peer-led discussion with no stage and no hierarchy—is bold. Usually, these events are just an opportunity for people to wear expensive sneakers and "network" (which is just a code word for collecting LinkedIn connections you'll never message). 

But if they actually stick to the "no pre-arranged questions" rule, we might hear some real talk. I want to hear the technical founders explain how they’re dealing with the massive brain drain. Every other week, my best devs are moving to London or Berlin. How do you maintain a robust fintech stack when your core team is constantly rotating?

![The numbers only look good if the backend holds up](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1551288049-bebda4e38f71?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Why I'm Skeptical (But Interested)
We’ve seen "exclusive" events before. They often turn into an echo chamber where everyone agrees that the "ecosystem is growing" while ignoring the fact that the average Nigerian is struggling to keep their head above water. 

But with guys like Somto from Piggyvest and the folks from Fincra and Zedcrest in the room, there's a chance for actual depth. These aren't people who just talk; they’ve built systems that handle billions of Naira. They know where the bodies are buried. 

If this conversation actually forces honesty about liquidity issues and the "No gree for anybody" attitude of the regulators, it might be worth the time. We don't need more "thought leadership." We need to know how to build stuff that doesn't break when 10,000 people try to pay for electricity at the same time on a Friday night.

![It all comes down to the lines of code and the people writing them](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Moving Past the Hype
Nigeria’s tech scene doesn't need more hype. We have enough of that in our music and our movies. What we need is infrastructure that works as hard as the people using it. From the hustle in the markets of Owerri to the tech hubs in Akure, people are looking for tools that actually solve the money problem, not more apps that look pretty but fail during a "liquidity crunch."

I’ll be watching to see if "Spotlight" actually shines a light on the mess, or if it’s just another room full of people in Lagos enjoying the air conditioning while the rest of the country deals with the bugs. 

Let's see if they actually choose honesty. I'm not holding my breath, but I’m definitely paying attention.
