My inverter is beep-beeping its final warning, the fan in my Gbagada workspace has just ground to a halt, and I’m staring at a Postgres database query that refuses to optimize. In the middle of trying to save my laptop battery, I came across this piece about nine Nigerians graduating from the Rotman School of Management in Canada who are gearing up to "bet big" on Africa.

Toheeb Daramola is looking at venture capital, Olanshile Aina wants to build alternative financing bridges, and Oyeshina Oyelade has handled billions in debt and equity. It sounds massive. But as someone whose hands are dirty with code every single day, I have to ask: how does this actually translate to the guy building a logistics app in Akure or a vendor trying to survive inflation in Onitsha?

### The Reality Gap Between Toronto and Gbagada

There is always a gap between the boardroom and the terminal. When you are sitting in Toronto studying case studies, "unlocking capital" sounds like a math problem. When you are on the ground here, unlocking capital feels like trying to find water in a desert with a fork.

![Working on code](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

Most local developers and small business owners aren't looking for a $150 million energy deal. We are looking for $5,000 to keep the AWS servers running, buy a reliable solar generator, and pay two junior devs so we don't burn out. The traditional banking system here doesn't understand us. If you go to a bank in Lagos to ask for a loan for a SaaS product, they ask for land documents. What land? My assets are sitting in a GitHub repository!

That is why Olanshile Aina's focus on alternative financing models caught my eye. We need funding structures that understand code, subscription models, and local transaction volumes, not just old-school collateral.

### Connect the Pipes, Don't Just Draw Diagrams

If these diaspora heavyweights want to make a dent, they need to look beyond the top 1% of startups. It is easy to back the hot new fintech that already has Y Combinator backing. It is much harder—and much more necessary—to build financial infrastructure for the rest of us.

![Street life and movement in Nigeria](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550005810-ca9161a0215a?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

We need a setup where a developer in Jos can easily access global micro-investments without having to register a Delaware flip-corp first. We need lines of credit that scale as our API call volume scales.

Toheeb Daramola's focus on connecting African founders to international networks is a step in the right direction. But those networks need to be democratized. Right now, if you didn't go to an Ivy League school or you don't have a warm intro to a partner, your pitch deck sits in an info@ mailbox forever while you battle Sapa.

### No Gree For Anybody, Just Keep Shipping

Despite the lack of easy capital, the "no gree for anybody" energy keeps the local ecosystem moving. We adapt. When the grid fails, we switch to solar. When payment APIs break, we write fallback logic to swap providers instantly. 

If this new wave of Rotman grads can actually build the bridges they talk about—connecting Canadian private markets directly to local builders without the usual three-month due diligence theater—then we might finally see things shift.

For now, my inverter is dead. I'm saving this file, closing my IDE, and heading out to find a charging spot before my next deployment. Let's see if that diaspora capital makes it to my street before the next fuel hike.
