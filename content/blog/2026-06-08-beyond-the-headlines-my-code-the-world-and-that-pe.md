Woke up today, grabbed my phone before the cold even left my toes (still experiencing some of those Jos morning chills even down here in Lagos, somehow). Pulled up the news feed and... man. Sometimes it feels like the whole world is just constantly on fire, metaphorically speaking, but today, some places are literally trading fire.

Top headlines shouting about missile strikes in the Middle East, back and forth. Then a 7.8 magnitude earthquake in the Philippines. My first thought wasn't about the diplomatic chess game, or the tectonic plates. It was, "Great, what's this going to do to global supply chains *now*?"

### When Global Chaos Hits Your Local Stack

See, for us builders here, especially in places like Akure or when you're grinding in a cramped space in Gbagada, these far-off events aren't just abstract political science lessons. They hit different. They affect the price of diesel for generators, which impacts hosting costs. They affect the exchange rate, making that new laptop or cloud subscription a luxury I have to re-evaluate every other week.

![A developer intently coding on a laptop, surrounded by lines of code on a screen.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

The news about Iran and Israel trading strikes? My mind immediately goes to oil prices. Higher oil means higher transport costs, higher everything. Suddenly, the "sapa" struggle for a bootstrapped startup just got a new layer. How do I keep my team motivated and paid when the cost of living keeps skyrocketing because of events happening thousands of miles away?

### Building for Resilience, Not Just Features

This just reinforces what I've always preached: we can't afford to build fragile systems, not just technically, but economically. If your product's entire existence hinges on stable global markets, you're in for a rude awakening. We need to build with lean principles ingrained, with an eye for local resilience.

I'm thinking about the payment solutions I'm working on. If a major global event disrupts international transfers, how can I ensure local transactions still flow smoothly? Or for the e-commerce platform I advised last month – how quickly can they pivot their sourcing if shipping routes get messed up? It's not just about clean code; it's about anticipating the world's unpredictable chaos and engineering around it.

It also makes me think about disaster recovery, even if it's not a local disaster. That earthquake in the Philippines? Beyond the human tragedy, it's a stark reminder that infrastructure can disappear in an instant. Are our data backups truly robust? Are we geographically distributed enough? These are the questions that keep me up, not who's bombing whom.

### The Nigerian Hustle, Uninterrupted

Despite the heavy global air, the energy in places like the Owerri bus park, or the bustling tech hubs around the country, it doesn't really dim. There's this "no gree for anybody" mindset that permeates everything here. You can't let the noise derail your grind. The problems are real, the need for solutions is urgent, and we're the ones who have to build them.

![Abstract image showing lines of code on a screen.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

So yeah, the world's a bit wild right now. But my focus remains squarely on the screen in front of me, the lines of code I'm pushing, and the users whose lives I'm trying to make a tiny bit easier. The global stage might be burning, but here, the servers are still humming, and the vision remains clear. Time to refactor that resilience into the core.
