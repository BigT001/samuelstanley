The morning air in Jos has a way of making you double-check your code and your life choices. It’s cold enough to make my old MacBook’s fan feel like a personal heater, but the headlines coming out of the US and Germany today are giving me a different kind of chill. While the world watches troops move out of Germany and "hostilities" being declared over in the Middle East, all I see is a massive shift in how global capital is going to move—and what that means for a guy trying to scale a product in the middle of a Naira fluctuation.

### The Problem with "Market Stability"

Whenever the US decides to pull 5,000 troops out of a place like Germany, it’s never just about the soldiers. It’s a signal that the old ways of doing business are being rewritten. For those of us building in Nigeria, especially if you're out in a tech hub like Akure or trying to keep a Gbagada workstation running on a backup generator, these "resets" feel like a bug in the production environment. 

We talk about "building for the next billion," but the infrastructure we use is still priced in dollars. Every time there’s a major geopolitical pivot, the FX market gets the jitters. My AWS bill doesn't care if the US is ending a conflict; it just knows that my purchasing power is doing a backflip. We’re out here trying to optimize queries to save a few cents because "Sapa" is real, and the global stage is moving the goalposts while we’re mid-sprint.

![Lines of code on a screen representing the complexity of local builds](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Regulation is the Ultimate Hotfix

Then you see the news about courts restricting health-related deliveries or colleges reshuffling leadership after some scandal. It reminds me of the chaos we face in the local fintech space. You spend three months perfecting a KYC flow, and then a regulator wakes up on the wrong side of the bed and decides that "Level 3" verification now requires a fingerprint from your first-grade teacher. 

Building tech in this climate means you have to be ready to push a hotfix at 2:00 AM because the "legal environment" changed while you were asleep. It’s not just about writing clean code; it’s about writing code that can survive a sudden policy U-turn. We’ve adopted a "No gree for anybody" mindset not because we’re stubborn, but because if you don’t push back against the chaos, your startup will be a memory by the next fiscal quarter.

### Shipping While the World Resets

I’ve spent the last few days looking at data visualizations for our user retention. It’s a bit like watching a graph of the global economy—plenty of peaks and valleys, mostly driven by things outside our control. 

![A graph showing success metrics and growth](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1551288049-bebda4e38f71?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

Whether it’s a college president retiring in New York or a troop withdrawal in Europe, the ripple effects eventually hit the funding pipelines. Investors get cautious. They start asking about "path to profitability" instead of "growth at all costs." Honestly? I’m here for it. The hype cycles in Lagos and beyond have been exhausting. I'd rather build something that works—something that solves a real problem for the trader in Onitsha or the student in Owerri—than chase a valuation that depends on the US President’s latest notification to Congress.

At the end of the day, the news is just noise. My job is to make sure the API stays up, the latency stays low, and we keep shipping. The world is going to do what it’s going to do. I’m just going to keep my head down and keep building until the next deployment.
