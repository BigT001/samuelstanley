You know that feeling when you're in the trenches, coding late, solving problems for users, maybe even fighting "sapa" to keep your team afloat, and then you hear all these big government pronouncements about the "booming digital economy"? It's like, *yeah, we know it's booming, we're the ones building the boom!* But then you wonder, are they even counting the right things?

That's why this news about NITDA trying to measure the *true value* of our digital economy actually caught my attention. Beyond just internet users and payment volumes – which, let's be honest, often feel like vanity metrics to us builders – they're talking about real impact: jobs, innovation, financial inclusion, public service delivery.

### The Problem with 'Fast-Growing' Without Data

For years, it's been a lot of hype. We see venture capital flowing, new apps launching every week, people in Onitsha paying for goods with USSD, and farmers in Akure using mobile money. The energy is undeniable. But when it comes to showing an investor, or even ourselves, exactly how much this translates to *sustainable growth* for the country, or for the average Nigerian's pocket, the data gets fuzzy.

NITDA Director General Kashifu Inuwa hit the nail on the head: we lack a harmonised system for measuring outcomes. It's tough to know what's actually working, where the gaps are, and where to put resources next if you don't have good data. As a product person, this is foundational. You don't just launch a feature and *hope* it works; you track, you measure, you iterate. Why should a whole national digital economy be any different?

### What This Means for Builders Like Us

Imagine if we had granular data showing exactly where digital tools are creating jobs in, say, Kaduna, or boosting small businesses in the chaotic energy of an Owerri bus park. Or maybe pinpointing regions where digital literacy is still painfully low despite broadband access.

This isn't just about government policy; it's about giving us, the developers and founders, a clearer map.
*   **Identify Opportunities**: If the data shows a specific sector or region is underserved, that's a signal for a new product, a new startup.
*   **Attract Investment**: Real, verifiable numbers about economic contribution are gold for attracting local and international investors who are tired of broad strokes. "Look, our fintech solutions added N20 billion to SME revenue in the South-South last year, demonstrably." That's a different conversation.
*   **Shape Policy**: If policymakers have solid evidence, they can create regulations and incentives that *actually* help us, instead of sometimes feeling like they're just guessing or reacting.

### My Cautious Optimism (Because, Nigeria)

Now, I'm a realist. "Measuring the true value" sounds fantastic on paper. But the execution? That's where the rubber meets the road.

First, **data quality**. Anyone who's tried to get reliable stats in Nigeria knows it's a marathon, not a sprint. The National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) being involved is a good sign, but capturing the sheer diversity and often informal nature of our digital activities across all six geopolitical zones is a monumental task. From the cold mornings in Jos to the bustling markets of Ibadan, digital adoption looks different everywhere.

Second, **accessibility and actionability**. Once this data is collected, will it be made easily available to the public, to researchers, and most importantly, to us, the builders, in an easily digestible format? And will it actually lead to tangible changes, not just another report that gathers dust? My hope is that the folks on the ground, the ones working from their Gbagada workstations or the tech hubs in Enugu, get to see and use this data.

![A graph showing upward trends, representing success and growth.](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1551288049-bebda4e38f71?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

Saidu Mohammed Kumo's point about our ability to track impact not keeping pace with expansion? That resonates deeply. We've been building by feel, by instinct, by the "no gree for anybody" mindset. Imagine what we could achieve with real, concrete insights.

This initiative, if done right, could be a game-changer. Not just for NITDA, but for every developer hustling, every founder taking a bet, and every Nigerian whose life is touched by technology. It's time we moved past just "counting internet users" to truly understanding the digital pulse of our nation. It's a massive undertaking, but hey, building great software is too, right? We tackle it one line of code, one feature, one bug at a time. This feels like a big feature rollout for the country.
