My neck is still stiff from debugging a stupid webhook timeout at 3 AM yesterday, and honestly, I am tired of the tech Twitter hype cycle. Every day, someone is telling you to dump your current stack and learn some brand-new JS framework that will probably be obsolete by next Friday. 

Then I ran into a post on DEV by Usman Ali. The guy has over 15 years under his belt building custom plugins, WooCommerce sites, and API integrations. 15 years. That means he was shipping code while half of today's tech influencers were still figuring out how to bypass their high school computer lab firewalls. 

It made me think about how much we look down on "boring" tech like WordPress, PHP, and basic database integrations, especially in our local ecosystem where everybody wants to build the next fintech unicorn.

### The Hype vs. The Ledger

If you walk into a Gbagada co-working space or hang out with developers in Akure, the conversation is always about Next.js, Docker, Kubernetes, and Web3. But if you talk to small business owners trying to sell clothes, book hotel rooms, or manage logistics, they do not care about your serverless architecture. 

They want a website that loads instantly on a patchy MTN network and lets customers pay with Paystack or Flutterwave without the page crashing. 

Usman’s experience is a reminder that there is a massive market for developers who actually know how to build custom, high-performing websites using tools that actually fit the client's budget. Custom theme development, WooCommerce booking systems, and performance optimization might not sound sexy at a tech meetup, but they pay the bills. When Sapa is knocking on your door, a solid client who needs a custom booking system built on PHP is a lifesaver.

![The reality of our local businesses and markets](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550005810-ca9161a0215a?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### The Trap of Over-Engineering

I remember a client from Lagos who insisted they needed a fully custom React-based web app for their real estate listings. We spent months dealing with complex state management, custom database schemas, and expensive hosting. Two months after launch, they realized they just wanted a simple blog and a contact form that worked. We could have built it in a week using a clean, optimized WordPress setup and saved them a lot of money and headache.

Writing raw code is great, but knowing *what* to build and *how* to keep it simple is the real mark of a senior engineer. 

Custom plugin development isn't just dragging and dropping templates in Elementor. It is about writing clean, secure PHP, understanding how databases handle queries under heavy traffic, and making sure your API endpoints do not leak customer data. It is real engineering.

### Shift Your Focus to Solving Real Problems

If you are a junior developer reading this, do not let anyone make you feel inferior because you are specializing in WordPress, WooCommerce, or PHP. 

Instead of chasing every shiny new tool, focus on:

- **Performance Optimization**: Can you make a bloated website load in under two seconds on a cheap smartphone?
- **Seamless Integrations**: Can you connect a local ERP system to an online store without breaking the database?
- **Security**: Can you write code that prevents basic SQL injection attacks and cross-site scripting?

If you can do these things, you will always have work. Usman's 15-year run with WP Rogers is proof that mastering the fundamentals of web commerce and client delivery is far more valuable than mastering the latest JavaScript trend. 

Let's stop building complex rocket ships when the client just needs a reliable bicycle to get to the market. Let me know what you think—are you sticking to your "boring" stack, or are you still chasing the hype?
