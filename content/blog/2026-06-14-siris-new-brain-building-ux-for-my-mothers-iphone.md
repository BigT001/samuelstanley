My mum called me from Akure last Tuesday. She was frustrated. Her sister had sent her some address details for a family wedding, but she couldn't remember if she saw them on WhatsApp, in an SMS, or inside a picture on Telegram. I spent fifteen minutes on the phone trying to guide her through the search bars of three different apps. 

It made me realize, yet again, how much we take modern user interfaces for granted. For those of us building products, navigating nested menus is second nature. For our parents, it’s a digital maze.

Apple's Siri update isn't just about competing with OpenAI or Google in some high-level tech war. As a developer who spends his days looking at user flows and wondering why people drop off, I see this as a massive step forward in accessibility and contextual UI.

### The Context Switching Trap

The real magic of the new Siri isn't just that it "understands language better." It’s that it finally has on-screen awareness and cross-app indexing.

Most older users don't think in terms of "apps." They think in terms of tasks. They don’t want to open WhatsApp, copy an address, close WhatsApp, open Apple Maps, paste the address, and hit navigate. They just want to get to the church.

Siri's ability to pull info across different apps—and actually understand what’s on the screen right now—removes that friction. If my mum is looking at a flyer sent to her on WhatsApp, she can just say, "Add this event to my calendar" or "How far is this place from me?" and Siri does the heavy lifting. 

From an execution standpoint, the implementation of App Intents to make this happen across third-party apps is the real win here. It shows they are thinking about how humans actually interact with devices, not just flashy AI demos.

### We Need This Energy in Nigerian Apps

Let's bring this home. Look at some of our local banking and utility apps here in Nigeria. The UX is frankly chaotic. We build these massive, bloated super-apps with fifty different options on the home screen, trying to capture every single transaction type. 

If a young developer in Gbagada is struggling to find where to buy power tokens on a newly redesigned app, imagine what our parents in Jos or Owerri are going through. They just want to send money or buy light without feeling like they are sitting for an exam.

We need to start building with this kind of conversational UX in mind. Imagine a local fintech app where instead of tapping through five nested menus to send money to a vendor, an older merchant at the market in Onitsha could just say, "Send fifteen thousand Naira to Mama Ngozi for the palm oil." No complicated menus, no tiny text to squint at.

### The Sceptical Developer View

Of course, I’m still a developer, so I have my reservations. Apple's on-device processing requires serious hardware. How much of this will actually run smoothly on the older, refurbished iPhones that are incredibly common in Nigeria? If these features are gated behind the latest premium chips, then the people who need this simplified interface the most won't even get to use it.

Still, the paradigm shift in how we approach interface design is real. Apple is pushing us away from "cluttered screens with too many buttons" toward "tell the device what you want, and let it figure out the steps."

It’s a design philosophy we should all start adopting in our own codebases. No more forcing users to adapt to our complex database structures. Let the tech do the adapting instead.
