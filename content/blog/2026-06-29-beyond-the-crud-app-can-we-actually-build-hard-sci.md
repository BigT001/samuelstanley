Honestly, I’m tired of seeing the same startup pitch deck. 

If I see one more "Uber for laundry" or another fintech app promising to help me save money in a currency that's depreciating faster than I can hit deploy, I might actually lose my mind. 

We’ve built a great ecosystem of software builders here in Nigeria, but let's be real: we've mostly been picking the low-hanging fruit. We write APIs, connect databases, and build pretty UIs. But when Open Startup announced they are shifting their entire focus to Africa’s "science economy" after supporting over 1,000 startups over the last decade, it made me stop and think. 

They are launching "The Science Road" and an early-stage fund called "Openers First" to back research-driven startups in healthcare, climate tech, and AI. They want labs, not just laptops. 

And as someone who spends his days staring at lines of code, I have both a lot of excitement and a healthy dose of anxiety about this.

![Coding on a laptop](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Software Is Easy. Labs Are Hard.

Let’s talk about execution. If my Node.js server crashes at 2:00 AM in my Gbagada workspace, I check the logs, fix the bug, push to GitHub, and redeploy. Within ten minutes, everything is fine. 

But what happens when your startup is trying to formulate a new local malaria treatment or build climate-resilient agricultural hardware? 

You can't just "patch" a failed lab experiment. You can’t run a biological assay on a free tier of AWS. The feedback loop in hard science is painfully slow, and the costs are brutal. 

In Nigeria, we have brilliant minds. Look at the tech grads coming out of FUTA in Akure or the researchers in cold laboratories in Jos. But they are fighting a system that isn't built for them. When you are constantly dealing with "Sapa" and trying to keep a lab generator running because the grid collapsed for the third time this month, doing deep scientific research feels like playing life on "hard mode."

### The "No Gree for Anybody" Approach to Deep Tech

Despite the hurdles, I love the sheer audacity of this pivot. 

We have spent years importing scientific solutions designed for temperate climates and Western infrastructure, then trying to patch them to work in Lagos or Owerri. It doesn't work. We need local scientific discoveries turned into commercial products. 

But here’s my worry as a builder: who is going to fund the "valley of death" for these science startups? 

Software startups can get to a minimum viable product (MVP) with a few thousand dollars and some sweat equity. A science startup might need three years and half a million dollars just to prove their compound doesn't kill cells in a petri dish. 

That is why Open Startup’s new investment vehicle, Openers First, is the most interesting part of this news. They aren't just giving advice; they are putting money on the table to bridge the gap between pre-seed and seed stages. 

![Lines of Code and Development](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Bridging the Gap Between Code and Chemistry

If this is going to work, we need a massive culture shift. 

Right now, our universities are silos. Academics publish papers just to get promoted, and the research sits in dust-covered folders in departmental offices. Meanwhile, devs like me are busy building e-commerce templates. 

We need to bring these two worlds together. Imagine an Akure software dev partnering with a biochemist from Ibadan to build an AI-driven diagnostic tool for local crop diseases. That’s where the real magic happens. 

It is going to take longer. It is going to be riskier. But if we want to build a resilient economy that actually solves our real-world problems, we have to stop playing it safe with simple software. It's time to roll up our sleeves, get dirty in the labs, and see what we can actually build when we push past the screen.
