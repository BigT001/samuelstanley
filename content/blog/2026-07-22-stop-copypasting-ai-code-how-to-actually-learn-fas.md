My inbox was flooded yesterday with a junior dev asking why his Next.js build was crashing on Vercel. One look at his codebase and I could tell what happened: he asked an AI to generate an entire authentication flow, copied it wholesale without reading a single line, and shoved it into production. When it broke, he didn't even know which file to open to start debugging.

That's the trap right there. AI tools in 2026 are sharper than ever, but if you're just using them as an automated copy-paste machine, you're building on sand. You might get the task done today, but you're setting yourself up for serious frustration down the road when you actually need to fix a critical issue under pressure.

### The "Copy-Paste Senior" Trap

We've all been tempted. You're exhausted, the power just went out for the third time in Akure, your inverter battery is at 15%, and you just need a script to clean up some database records before your laptop dies. Prompting ChatGPT or Claude to "write this script for me" feels like a lifesaver.

And honestly? For repetitive boilerplate, go for it. But when you're trying to *learn* a new stack—say, picking up Rust or trying to understand complex database indexing—letting the model do all the thinking for you is lethal to your growth.

If you don't fight with the logic at least a little bit, your brain doesn't retain it. You end up with zero muscle memory. The moment you're offline or in a technical interview where you can't access an LLM, the illusion shatters.

### Treat AI Like a Persistent Senior Dev (Who Doesn't Mind Dumb Questions)

The real unlock isn't asking AI for full answers. It's asking AI for *friction*.

Instead of sending a prompt like:
> "Write a Python script to scrape this site."

Flip the prompt completely. Try this instead:
> "I'm trying to scrape this page using BeautifulSoup, but I'm stuck on handling pagination. Don't write the full code for me. Explain the logic pattern, give me a tiny exercise to practice it, and point out two edge cases I should watch out for."

When I was trying to get my head around asynchronous programming in Python a while back, I used to ask Claude to act like an aggressive code reviewer. I'd paste my ugly, working code and say: "Roast this. Tell me where I'm blocking the event loop like I'm a complete novice."

It completely shifted my understanding. It wasn't about getting generated scripts; it was about getting an instant, personalized feedback loop—the kind you usually only get if you're lucky enough to pair-program with an experienced tech lead at a hub in Gbagada.

### The Feynman Trick via Prompt Engineering

A recent piece on TechCity mentioned the Feynman Technique—explaining something simply to prove you actually understand it. AI makes this insanely easy to pull off on your own without needing to bother your colleagues.

Here is my exact workflow when I'm tackling a tricky concept:

1. **Read the raw docs or watch a short tutorial.** (No shortcuts here; absorb the actual source material).
2. **Explain it back to the AI in my own words.** I open a fresh chat and write: *"I just learned about database transactions and ACID compliance. Here is how I understand it in my own words: [insert my rough explanation]. Tell me what I got wrong, what I missed, and give me a real-world edge case where this breaks."*
3. **Ask for an active test.** I follow up with: *"Create 3 scenario-based questions where I have to choose between Postgres transactions or handle locking manually. Don't give me the answers until I respond."*

Testing yourself this way hits way harder than passive reading. It burns the concept into your memory so you aren't burning expensive MTN data re-learning the exact same topic three weeks later.

### Speed Means Nothing Without Mastery

Building tech products in Nigeria means navigating unpredictable infrastructure, tight deadlines, and high expectations. We like shortcuts—it's in our DNA to hack solutions together quickly and keep moving. But fast learning isn't about skipping steps; it's about compressing the time it takes to get feedback.

If you're using AI to dodge the heavy lifting of understanding logic, you aren't learning faster. You're just outsourcing your thinking.

Next time you open your AI chat window, don't treat it like a vendor delivering a finished product. Treat it like a mentor standing over your shoulder, forcing you to write better code yourself. That's how you actually level up.
