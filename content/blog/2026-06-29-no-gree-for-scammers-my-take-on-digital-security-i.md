Man, it's been a month. Internet Safety Month just wrapped up, and I was reading a recap from TechCityNG – ten lessons everyone should remember. And honestly, as someone building stuff here, pushing code from my Gbagada workstation, the points hit different. It's not just theoretical; it's daily life, daily struggle, daily wahala for our users.

The article talks about cybercriminals using AI for phishing, deepfakes, fake websites in 2026. Yeah, no kidding. Here, "wash-wash" has gone digital, and these guys are getting *sophisticated*. It’s a constant battle, not just for the user but for us, the builders, to keep our platforms safe and trustworthy.

![A person working on a laptop with lines of code visible, representing the developer's perspective](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

### Beyond the Click: The Developer's Burden

"Think Before You Click" – that’s lesson one. Classic. Phishing emails, fake WhatsApp links, social media scams. They all bait you to click first, think later. But from a developer's perspective, this is a heavy lift. How do you design a UI that nudges a user to *pause*? How do you implement robust link scanning, especially when the bad guys are using AI to craft messages that look so legit, even my auntie, who sends me prayer points every morning, could fall for it?

We're constantly working on backend checks, domain verification, and building in warning systems. But the human element, that "sense of urgency" the scammers create, is tough to crack. It reminds me of those "Awoof data" messages that look so real. You just want to scream: "It's a scam, dear!"

### Password Managers? My Guy, We Need 2FA!

Lesson two: "Strong Passwords Are Only the Beginning." Unique passwords, password managers, and two-factor authentication (2FA). This one is non-negotiable for me. As a founder, when we're building a new payment platform or a data analytics tool, 2FA isn't an add-on; it's foundational.

Integrating 2FA can be a bit of a dance with different providers and user flows, but it's worth every line of code. Because even if some bad actor gets a password – maybe from a data breach on *another* platform – that 2FA is the brick wall. We preach it to our users, but we also have to make it *easy* for them to set up and use. No one wants a complicated security setup when they just want to send money to their parents in Akure or pay for light tokens.

### Scams Evolve: The "Sharp Guy" Problem Goes Digital

The article notes that "Scams Are More Convincing Than Ever" – fake investments, AI-generated phishing, deepfakes, remote work scams. This is where it gets really personal for us in Nigeria. "Wash-wash" has always been a thing, but now it's got a tech upgrade. Those fake investment platforms promising 50% ROI in 3 days? They look sleek now, complete with testimonials and "secure certificates." It’s tempting, especially with the current economic realities pushing people to find quick ways to beat *sapa*.

As product builders, our responsibility extends beyond just our platform. We need to be aware of the ecosystem. How do we build trust when the landscape is littered with these sophisticated traps? How do we educate users without sounding preachy? It's about designing clear reporting mechanisms and staying agile to counter these new forms of fraud. The 'no gree for anybody' mindset needs to extend to scammers too.

### Privacy is More Than Incognito Mode, It's Good UX

"Privacy Starts With Everyday Habits." Reviewing app permissions, limiting location sharing, regular updates. Most people think Incognito Mode is some magic cloak. Nah, it's just basic browser hygiene. For us devs, this means building apps that are transparent about data use. Don't hide permission requests deep in settings. Make it obvious.

When you're designing an app, every permission prompt is a trust signal. Do you *really* need access to their contacts for a simple utility app? Probably not. It's a continuous balancing act between enhancing user experience with data and respecting their digital boundaries. And please, for the love of stable builds, *update your devices*. Those security patches are not just for show!

### My People Need Support – Especially the OGs

"Your Parents and Older Relatives Need Support Too." This one hit home. My mum still calls me when she gets a strange SMS, asking if it's "real." My uncle almost fell for a fake prize notification last year. Technology moves so fast, and scammers often target the less tech-savvy.

It's not about criticizing them; it's about patience. We build products, but we also build community. And that means explaining, simply, why they shouldn't click that link or give out their ATM PIN over the phone. Maybe our platforms need simpler, more guided security settings for this demographic. It's a UX challenge that's more about empathy than elegant code.

### Public Wi-Fi: The Free Lunch Trap

"Public Wi-Fi Requires Extra Caution." That free Wi-Fi at the airport or that local café in Owerri? Convenient, yes. Secure? Probably not. I've heard stories. I myself have been tempted. But accessing your bank app or making sensitive transactions on open networks is just asking for trouble.

As developers, we need to assume our users *will* use our apps on insecure networks. This means strong encryption (HTTPS everywhere!), robust session management, and ideally, nudging users towards VPNs for sensitive transactions. It's about building layers of security so even if the network is compromised, the data isn't easily readable.

### Your Phone, Your Habits: The Human Firewall

Finally, "Your Phone Is Only as Secure as Your Habits." Android or iPhone, it doesn't matter. Updates, official app stores, screen locks. The device itself is just hardware. The user is the final firewall.

As someone who builds mobile apps, I constantly push updates. Sometimes, I dread the feedback ("Stanley, your update broke my XYZ!"). But most times, these updates include critical security fixes. We also try to guide users towards official stores, not those sketchy APK sites that promise premium features for free. Because at the end of the day, no matter how good our code is, if the user downloads malware or leaves their phone unlocked at a bus park, our work is compromised.

It's a lot to take in, isn't it? Internet safety isn't some abstract concept; it's part of the daily grind for us building tech in Nigeria. It's about earning trust, protecting our users from increasingly clever adversaries, and constantly adapting. The work never stops. And neither should our vigilance.
