It’s 2:15 AM. The neighborhood in Gbagada is quiet, save for the low, rhythmic rumble of a neighbor's generator that should have been turned off hours ago. I’m lying in bed, my face illuminated by the harsh blue light of my iPhone. I only opened it to check if a late-night deployment on our staging server went through. 

Now, forty-five minutes later, I’ve somehow transitioned from AWS consoles to arguing on Twitter about a new JavaScript framework, before finally landing on a rabbit hole of short videos. My eyes burn, my brain is buzzing, and I know tomorrow’s 9 AM standup is going to be brutal. 

We’ve all seen the articles. Bukola Oyeyemi’s piece on TechCity about the phone habits ruining our sleep in 2026 hit a little too close to home. But reading it as a developer makes you look at the problem through a very specific lens: we are literally the ones building the systems that are keeping us awake.

### The Hypocrisy of "User Retention"

When we sit down to design apps, we talk about "frictionless experiences." We write clean pagination queries, implement lazy loading, and optimize WebSockets so that content flows into a user's feed without a single millisecond of delay. 

![Coding on a laptop late at night](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517694712282-14f494bc6f0e?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

The "infinite scroll" is a technical marvel but a psychological disaster. By removing the natural friction of clicking "Next Page," we’ve eliminated the pause button for the human brain. I spent two weeks last year optimizing the feed algorithm for a local client's e-commerce platform, trying to keep users hooked for just three minutes longer. Now, I’m the victim of my own industry's cleverness. 

When you spend your day building feedback loops, you realize how easily your own biology is hacked by them. That dopamine hit of a pull request approval or a new user signup notification is the exact same trigger that keeps you scrolling through depressing news cycles when you should be resting.

### The Slack, Sapa, and Notification Anxiety

It’s not just social media. For those of us building tech in Nigeria, the pressure to always be "on" is intense. Between managing offshore clients in different time zones and trying to outrun inflation—our collective friend, Sapa—the anxiety is real. 

If my phone buzzes at midnight, my immediate thought isn't "Oh, a fun text." It’s "Is the database down?" or "Did the payment gateway API fail again?" 

![Lines of code on a dark screen](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1555066931-4365d14bab8c?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

Even if you don't open the message, just seeing that little LED light flash or hearing the vibration triggers a spike of cortisol. We live in a state of anticipatory stress. We sleep with one eye open because we’re afraid of missing that one critical alert that could cost us a contract or ruin a product launch. 

### Rewriting My Bedtime Routine (The Developer Way)

If my code has a bug, I don't just complain about it; I write a patch. It’s time to patch my sleep routine. 

I’ve started treating my sleep hygiene like a system deployment. Here is the stack I'm implementing to protect my sanity:

* **The Router Kill-Switch:** I set up my home router to automatically disable Wi-Fi access for my personal devices at 11 PM. If I want to scroll, I have to manually use my mobile data, and in this economy, burning through expensive data plans just to watch mindless videos adds enough friction to make me put the phone down.
* **Aggressive Focus Filters:** iOS and Android have gotten really good at this. I’ve configured my "Sleep Focus" to block everything except calls from my immediate family and PagerDuty (for actual server emergencies). Slack, WhatsApp, and Gmail are completely muted after 10 PM. No exceptions.
* **Analogue Debugging:** I’ve replaced the pre-sleep screen time with a physical paperback book. No backlights, no notifications, no interactive UI. Just ink on paper. 

![A quiet scene in Nigeria, reflecting on the day](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1550005810-ca9161a0215a?q=80&w=1200&auto=format&fit=crop)

The tech ecosystem in Nigeria moves at a breakneck pace. Whether you’re hustling in Gbagada, debugging in a cold room in Jos, or running a tech hub in Akure, you cannot build great things if your brain is operating on three hours of fractured sleep. 

We need to learn to shut down our personal servers every night. The code will still be there tomorrow. The servers will survive. Your health might not.
